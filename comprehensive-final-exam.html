Read This First

This is a drawing-based exam. Your drawings carry the answer. Words alone, even correct words, will not earn full credit.

You will be asked to integrate structure with function across multiple systems. Expect to draw nephrons, capillary beds, the cardiac cycle, ventilation curves, the GI tract, acid-base buffer responses. Expect to label, annotate, and explain mechanisms inside the drawing itself.

If you only studied the words and not the visuals, you are not ready. Practice drawing each system from memory before the exam opens.

What the Exam Covers

Everything from Exam 2 forward is fair game. The exam is comprehensive across five systems:

System 1

Cardiovascular

Cardiac cycle, ECG, pressure-volume loops, hemodynamics, blood pressure regulation, capillary dynamics, autoregulation.

System 2

Respiratory

Pulmonary mechanics, ventilation, gas exchange, oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation, CO2 transport, control of breathing.

System 3

Renal

Filtration, reabsorption, secretion at each nephron segment, countercurrent multiplier, hormonal regulation (ADH, aldosterone, RAAS, ANP, PTH), JGA.

System 4

Acid-Base, Fluid & Electrolytes

ABG interpretation, the four primary disorders, compensation mechanisms, body fluid compartments, sodium, potassium, calcium, water balance.

System 5

Gastrointestinal

GI anatomy and physiology integrated. Mechanical and chemical digestion, secretion (HCl, enzymes, bile, bicarbonate), absorption mechanisms, GI hormones (gastrin, secretin, CCK, GIP, motilin), motility patterns.

Integration is the Point

Expect questions that cross systems. A question on hypovolemic shock pulls cardiovascular, renal, and acid-base. A question on a patient vomiting for three days pulls GI, fluid balance, acid-base, and renal compensation. A question on COPD pulls respiratory and acid-base. Study each system, then study the connections.

Format & Expectations

Exam Structure

3 drawing-based questions, each worth 33.3% of the exam. Questions are integrative and may pull from any of the five systems: cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, acid-base & fluid/electrolytes, and GI. All five systems are fair game.

  • Drawing-based: Each of the 3 questions requires a labeled drawing or diagram as the primary answer. Same approach as your most recent exam.
  • Integration questions: Expect clinical scenarios that span multiple systems within a single question. A single question may pull cardiovascular, renal, and acid-base together.
  • Free-response style: No multiple choice. Each answer must be drawn, labeled, and explained.
  • Mechanism-driven: Saying what something does is not enough. You must show how it does it. Transporters, hormones, pressure gradients, feedback loops should appear inside your drawings.
  • Equal weight: Each question is worth 33.3% of your exam grade. Do not skip or rush any one of them.
  • Plan to spend several focused sessions: Block out 6 to 10 hours total across the week. Cramming this exam in one sitting will not work.
