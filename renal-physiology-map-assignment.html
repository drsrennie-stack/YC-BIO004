Read This First

This assignment is focused on physiology, but you cannot separate structure from function.

You are still responsible for knowing the renal anatomy. The countercurrent multiplier means nothing if you don't know where the loop of Henle sits. ADH cannot work without principal cells in the collecting duct. Renin cannot be released without granular cells on the afferent arteriole. The physiology lives on top of the anatomy. Study both.

What You Will Do

You will build a single-page concept map of renal physiology that organizes glomerular filtration, tubular reabsorption, tubular secretion, and hormonal regulation across the nephron. The goal is integration on one page. By the end you should be able to point to any segment and say what is being moved, by what mechanism, in which direction, and what hormone is regulating it.

Drawings carry the mechanism. A label without a process, or a process without a location, will not earn full credit.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this activity, you should be able to:

  1. Describe the three filtration barriers and what determines net filtration pressure.
  2. Map reabsorption and secretion at each nephron segment with the major transporters.
  3. Explain the countercurrent multiplier in the loop of Henle and the role of the vasa recta.
  4. Map the major hormones (ADH, aldosterone, ANP, PTH, RAAS) to stimulus, source, target, and effect.
  5. Connect tubuloglomerular feedback and the JGA to GFR autoregulation and renin release.

Instructions

  1. Plan before you draw. One page fills up fast. Sketch the nephron lightly first, then plan where each system of annotations will go before committing.
  2. Draw the nephron with all segments in order: glomerulus and Bowman capsule, PCT, descending limb, ascending limb, DCT, collecting duct. Add the blood supply (afferent arteriole, glomerular capillaries, efferent arteriole, peritubular capillaries, vasa recta).
  3. Annotate filtration: filtration barriers, the three Starling forces, GFR.
  4. Annotate reabsorption and secretion at each segment. Identify what is moved, in what direction, by what transporter or mechanism. Note the percentage reabsorbed at each segment where relevant.
  5. Show the countercurrent system: how the loop of Henle creates the medullary concentration gradient, how the vasa recta preserves it.
  6. Map the hormones: ADH, aldosterone, ANP, PTH. Show stimulus, source, target site on the nephron, and effect. Add the RAAS sequence.
  7. Show the JGA: macula densa, granular cells. Connect to tubuloglomerular feedback and renin release.
  8. Submit a clear photo or scan of your completed one-page map. Make sure all labels are legible.
